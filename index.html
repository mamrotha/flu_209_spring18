Introduction

In the year of 2017-2018, nearly 12,000 people have been hospitalized with confirmed cases of flu, an increase of 3,000 in just one week, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). If you're not sick, there's a good chance some of your friends, family members, or colleagues are.

CDC monitors certain key flu indicators (for example, outpatient visits of influenza-like illness (ILI), the results of laboratory testing and reports of flu hospitalizations and deaths). This season, ILI went above baseline in late November and remained elevated through February 2018.

Geographic Pattern

The CDC stated that as of January, every part of the continental U.S. showed “widespread” flu activity in this flu season. Comparing 2017-18 to the past, although the geographical pattern seems similar, the magnitude of flu spread is much higher.

So Why is 2017-2018 Season is Bad?

Hospitalized Patients by Age Group

Overall hospitalizations are significantly higher than what officials have normally seen this time of year since CDC began using this tracking system in 2010. Typically, flu epidemics quickly spread among young and elder patients due to their weaker immune system.

Many suspect that 2017-18 epidemic’s severity is due to the age group that are being affected. Although we do see a high percentage of elderly patients, this is nothing new compared to the past years.
